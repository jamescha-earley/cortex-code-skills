# James Cha-Earley — LinkedIn Voice Guide

Style patterns derived from actual LinkedIn posts. Use these as constraints when drafting.

## Opener

- Often leads with an emoji followed by a short hook
- Conversational and direct: "If you haven't already heard of...", "Everyone's talking about X. But where do you actually start?"
- Can address the audience directly: "Heads up for anyone..."
- Avoids generic "Excited to announce" / "Thrilled to share" openers

## Tone

- Casual-professional. Reads like a smart colleague talking, not a press release.
- Uses first person. Shares personal opinions and reactions: "The part I'm most interested in is...", "I am THRILLED to be sharing the stage with her"
- Comfortable with humor and personality: "Pirates may or may not be involved."
- Not afraid to be blunt: "Where have you been?"
- Avoids corporate jargon and stiff phrasing

## Structure

- Short paragraphs separated by line breaks — rarely more than 2-3 sentences per block
- Technical details are woven into prose, not presented as formal bullet lists
- When listing things, uses a casual inline style: "Python, SQL, natural language runbooks, Snowpark Container Services, Cortex AI — the full stack."
- Posts are typically 80-150 words. Occasionally longer for meaty topics but never bloated.

## Engagement

- Almost always ends with a question or call to action directed at the audience: "Who's going to be there?", "What would you build?", "Would love to hear what you're looking to build — drop a comment!"
- Sometimes tags people or references them by name when relevant

## Hashtags

- 3-5 hashtags at the bottom, separated from the body
- Consistent use of #Snowflake, then topic-specific tags
- Example: #Snowflake #AI #CortexAI #DataEngineering

## Things to avoid

- Wall-of-text paragraphs
- Formal announcement tone ("We are pleased to inform you...")
- Bold-first bullet lists
- Passive voice
- Generic filler ("In today's rapidly evolving landscape...")
